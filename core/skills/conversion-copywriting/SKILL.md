# Conversion Copywriting

Conversion copy isn't writing prettier sentences — it's choosing the smallest set of sentences that move a reader from "I'm here" to "I'll do the thing." Every word competes for the click.

## When to use

- Landing page for a product, feature, or campaign
- Email — sales sequence, lifecycle, re-engagement, launch announcement
- Ad copy (search, social, display)
- Blog post that exists to drive a downstream action (signup, lead, purchase)
- Sales page, pricing page, comparison page
- Any copy with a measurable success metric beyond "people read it"

Skip for: internal docs, neutral reference content, technical writing where persuasion is off-mission, brand storytelling without a CTA.

## The five questions to answer before writing a word

1. **Who is this reader?** Not "marketers" — "an ops manager at a 50-person SaaS company who just got asked to cut tooling cost 20%."
2. **What do they believe right now?** Their current story about themselves and the problem.
3. **What do we want them to do?** One action. Not three. Not a menu.
4. **What's stopping them?** The objections, doubts, alternatives in their head.
5. **What's the proof?** What evidence shifts belief — testimonial, data, demo, guarantee.

If you can't answer all five in one sentence each, copy isn't the bottleneck — research is.

## Frameworks (use the one that fits, not all of them)

### PAS — Problem / Agitate / Solution
Best for pain-driven decisions. Name the pain specifically, sit with it for a beat, present the solution. Works when the reader is already feeling the problem.

### AIDA — Attention / Interest / Desire / Action
Best for cold traffic. Hook → relevant context → why it matters to them → what to do.

### Before / After / Bridge
Best for transformation stories. Where you are → where you could be → the bridge that gets you there.

### FAB — Features / Advantages / Benefits
Best for feature pages. What it is → what it does → what that means for the reader. Most copy stops at features. Conversion copy ends at benefits.

### JTBD framing — Job to be done
Best when the product replaces a different solution (or no solution). Frame around the job, not the product.

## Anatomy of a converting page

| Section | Job |
|---------|-----|
| Hero headline | Earn the next 5 seconds |
| Sub-headline | Specify who and what |
| Lead visual | Show the outcome, not the interface |
| Primary CTA (above fold) | Make the action obvious |
| Proof block | Names, logos, numbers — fast |
| Problem articulation | Reader thinks "yes, that's me" |
| Solution explanation | Three benefits, ordered by importance |
| Objection handlers | The "but what about..." section |
| Secondary CTA | Catch readers who needed more |
| Risk reversal | Money-back, free trial, no card, etc. |
| Final CTA | The "I'm ready now" close |

Every page won't have every section. But every page should know which sections it's deliberately skipping.

## Headlines — the highest-leverage line

The headline does ~80% of the work. Bad headline = nothing else gets read. Test ruthlessly.

Patterns that consistently work:
- **Specific outcome + timeframe** — "Cut your CI bill 40% in one afternoon"
- **Named pain + named relief** — "Stop chasing payments. Get paid on time."
- **Before/after** — "From spreadsheet chaos to one source of truth"
- **Curiosity gap with payoff** — "The mistake every Postgres team makes (and the one-line fix)"
- **Direct address with stakes** — "Your onboarding is leaking money. Here's the fix."

Patterns that consistently underperform:
- Clever wordplay nobody decodes in 2 seconds
- "Welcome to [Brand]" — wastes the most valuable line on the page
- Vague benefit ("Better collaboration") with no who, what, or how

## Voice rules

- **Specific beats clever.** "Save $1,200/month" beats "Big savings."
- **You / your beats we / our.** It's about the reader.
- **Concrete beats abstract.** "300 invoices in 4 minutes" beats "fast invoicing."
- **Active beats passive.** Subject does the verb.
- **Read it out loud.** If it sounds like a press release, rewrite.
- **Cut every word that doesn't earn its space.** Conversion copy is short on average for a reason.

## Calls to action

- One primary action per page. Secondary actions get less visual weight.
- Action verb + outcome: "Start free" beats "Submit." "Get my plan" beats "Click here."
- Reduce friction in the words: "Free, no credit card" right by the button defangs hesitation.
- Repeat the CTA. People scroll, scan, leave, return. The button after each section catches each.

## Common mistakes

- Writing about the product instead of the reader
- Listing features as if they sell themselves
- Hedging language: "We aim to help you potentially save..." — cut every soft word
- One CTA at the bottom of a long page (most readers leave before getting there)
- Stock photos of generic people pretending to laugh at a laptop
- Headline that requires the sub-headline to explain what the product is
- Stacking testimonials with no specifics ("Great product! — Happy Customer")
- Writing for the founder's friends instead of the actual buyer
- "Game-changing", "revolutionary", "synergy" — banned-word territory

## Common mistakes — emails specifically

- Subject line that works as the headline ≠ subject line that gets opened
- One email trying to do three jobs (announce + educate + sell)
- Hero image instead of a sentence — many clients block images by default
- Long form when a 3-line nudge would convert better
- No P.S. — the second-most-read line in any email

## Quick reference — diagnostic for low conversion

| Symptom | Likely cause |
|---------|--------------|
| High traffic, low click | Headline / hero mismatch with traffic source |
| High click, low signup | Friction in form / onboarding, not copy |
| Reads, doesn't act | Missing urgency, missing risk reversal, vague CTA |
| Bouncing fast | Lead doesn't match what was promised in the ad |
| "Looks great but I don't get it" | Buried what-it-is, over-rotated on benefits |

## Output checklist before publishing

- [ ] One reader, one action, named explicitly
- [ ] Headline survives the 5-second test
- [ ] Three benefits, ordered, each with proof
- [ ] CTA repeated, never ambiguous
- [ ] Specifics replace adjectives
- [ ] Objections addressed before they're raised
- [ ] Read out loud — sounds human
- [ ] Mobile preview — heroic on desktop, broken on phone is most of the audience

## Common Rationalizations

When you're tempted to skip this skill, watch for these excuses:

| Excuse | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| "I'll write it 'professionally' and edit later" | 'Professional' usually means jargon-heavy + lifeless. Edit toward specificity from draft 1, not after legal review. |
| "This is a simple change, doesn't need <skill>" | Bugs hide in simple changes too — DAPLab data shows 41% of agentic-LLM failures land in 'trivial' diffs. |
| "I already know the answer" | Confirmation bias — the skill exists to surface what you didn't think of, not to repeat what you did. |
| "Time pressure, skip just this once" | Tech debt compounds; 5 minutes saved at write time costs 50 minutes of debugging later. |

Default response when rationalizing: run the skill anyway. Cost of running it is bounded; cost of skipping when you needed it is not.
